## Abstract

White holes—mathematically allowed by general relativity but never observed—have remained unexplained for over 50 years. Standard interpretations treat them as time-reversed black holes, providing no formation mechanism and predicting ubiquity contradicting observations. We propose a fundamentally different explanation: **white holes are spontaneous ruptures in boundaries separating universes or dimensions, releasing accumulated trapped information.**

This mechanism applies universally to any framework featuring multiple universes or extra dimensions: brane cosmology, many-worlds interpretation, string theory compactifications, or phase-separated dual universes. Formation requires two conditions: (1) critical accumulation of information trapped at black hole event horizons, transported by boundary waves from stellar phenomena over cosmic time; (2) geometric weak points where boundary separation is minimized.

Mathematical formulation yields four coupled equations governing: information residue accumulation, formation conditions, rupture dynamics, and mass expulsion. The mechanism naturally explains white hole rarity (formation timescale ~10⁶-10⁹ years), instability (exotic boundary material, self-limiting rupture), brief duration (seconds to minutes), and absence of observations (extreme rarity + unpredictable timing + brief visibility).

Testable predictions include: spatial distribution correlated with black hole density and stellar activity; temporal characteristics distinguishing rupture types; spectroscopic signatures of exotic matter; morphological classification (spherical, pulsed, jet-like, chaotic). Candidate observations include Fast Radio Bursts, orphan gamma-ray bursts, and anomalous transients lacking clear progenitors.

Unlike phenomenological models, this framework provides a mechanistic explanation falsifiable within the next decade through transient surveys (LSST, SKA, JWST) and multi-messenger astronomy. If confirmed, white hole detection would provide direct empirical evidence for multi-universe structure.

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The White Hole Problem

White holes represent one of theoretical physics' most persistent puzzles. Mathematically allowed as time-reversed solutions to Einstein's field equations, white holes should—in principle—exist as black holes' inverse: horizons from which matter and energy can only escape, never enter (Hawking & Ellis 1973).

Yet despite 50+ years of astronomical surveys covering electromagnetic spectrum from radio to gamma rays, including gravitational wave observations, **no white hole has been definitively detected** (Barceló et al. 2011). This absence poses fundamental questions:

**Question 1: Formation**  
How do white holes come into existence? Time-reversal of black hole formation (stellar collapse run backwards) is causally incoherent.

**Question 2: Stability**  
Are white holes stable? Theoretical analyses suggest extreme instability—perturbations cause rapid collapse (Eardley 1974).

**Question 3: Observability**  
If white holes exist, why have we never seen one? Should they not be as common as black holes?

**Question 4: Matter Source**  
What would white holes expel? From where does this matter originate?

Standard astrophysics provides no satisfactory answers. White holes remain theoretical curiosities disconnected from observable reality.

### 1.2 Previous Approaches

Several proposals have attempted to explain white holes:

**Time-Reversed Black Holes (Penrose 1965, Hawking 1975):**  
White holes as mathematical time-reversals of black hole formation.  
**Problem:** No physical formation mechanism. Causally incoherent.

**Primordial White Holes (Novikov 1964, Kardashev et al. 1967):**  
White holes formed in early universe, present since Big Bang.  
**Problem:** Predicts ubiquitous observation. Conflicts with non-detection.

**Black-to-White Transition (Haggard & Rovelli 2015):**  
Black holes transition to white holes via quantum gravity effects.  
**Problem:** Requires full quantum gravity theory (unavailable). Long timescales (>10^67 years).

**White Holes as Vacuum Energy (Retter & Heller 2012):**  
White holes connected to dark energy.  
**Problem:** Speculative. No clear mechanism or predictions.

None provides: (1) concrete formation mechanism, (2) explanation for non-observation, (3) testable predictions.

### 1.3 Our Proposal

We propose white holes are **boundary rupture phenomena** occurring at interfaces between universes or dimensions. This mechanism:

✓ Applies universally to any multi-universe framework  
✓ Explains rarity (formation timescale ~10⁶-10⁹ years)  
✓ Explains instability (exotic boundary material)  
✓ Explains brief duration (self-limiting rupture)  
✓ Makes specific, testable predictions  

The proposal emerged from work on the Phase-Dual Cascade Cosmology (PDCC), a framework explaining JWST early structures, Hubble tension, and dark phenomena through phase-separated dual universes (Marcelo 2025). However, the white hole mechanism is **more general**, applying to:

- Brane cosmology (Randall-Sundrum, ekpyrotic models)
- Large extra dimensions (ADD, DGP models)
- String theory compactifications
- Many-worlds quantum mechanics
- Any scenario with multiple universes/dimensions

This universality suggests the mechanism may represent fundamental physics of multi-universe structures rather than model-specific prediction.

## 2. Universal Mechanism: Boundary Rupture Dynamics

### 2.1 Core Concept

The mechanism requires three ingredients present in any multi-universe framework:

**Ingredient 1: Multiple Universes/Dimensions**  
At least two distinct regions of spacetime (universes, branes, dimensions, quantum branches) separated by some structure (boundary, bulk, interface, branch point).

**Ingredient 2: Information Transfer Zones**  
Locations where information/matter can cross between regions. In gravitational frameworks: black holes. In quantum frameworks: decoherence events or measurement interactions.

**Ingredient 3: Energetic Phenomena**  
Events creating stress/waves in the separating structure: supernovae, stellar collisions, phase transitions, quantum fluctuations, etc.

These three ingredients, combined with sufficient time, lead inevitably to white hole formation.

### 2.2 The Four-Stage Process

**Stage 1: Information Trapping (10⁶-10⁹ years)**

At information transfer zones (e.g., black hole event horizons), transfer efficiency is not perfect. A small fraction (~0.001-0.01%) of information becomes **trapped in the boundary material itself**—neither fully transferred nor remaining in origin universe.

*Physical picture:* Imagine information as "water" trying to cross a membrane. Most flows through, but tiny droplets get stuck in membrane pores.

This trapped information accumulates over cosmic time. With billions of black holes each trapping tiny fractions, total accumulation becomes significant.

**Stage 2: Wave Transport (Ongoing)**

Energetic phenomena create waves/stress patterns in the boundary structure. These waves propagate, carrying trapped information from origin sites to new locations.

*Different phenomena create different wave types:*
- Supernovae: Massive spherical shock waves
- Jets (black holes, pulsars): Sustained directional beams
- Mergers: Gravitational wave-induced stress
- Periodic sources: Rhythmic pulses

Over millions of years, complex interference patterns develop as waves from multiple sources overlap constructively and destructively.

**Stage 3: Critical Accumulation (Rare)**

Wave interference occasionally creates regions where:
1. Trapped information converges to high density
2. Boundary has geometric weak point (local thinning)

When **both conditions coincide**—high information density at weak point—the boundary approaches instability.

*Analogy:* Water pressure building behind a dam with a thin spot. Most of dam is strong (thick boundary). But at thin spot with high pressure, failure becomes possible.

**Stage 4: Rupture and Expulsion (Seconds to Minutes)**

At critical density, boundary undergoes catastrophic rupture:

1. Initial perturbation slightly thins weak point
2. Increased stress from accumulated information
3. Positive feedback: thinning → more stress → more thinning
4. Exponential instability growth
5. Explosive rupture: boundary "tears" open
6. Violent expulsion of accumulated material
7. Rapid resealing as pressure drops

**This transient rupture-expulsion-resealing event is a white hole.**

### 2.3 Why This Explains Observed White Hole Properties

**Property 1: Extreme Rarity**

Formation requires simultaneous satisfaction of two unlikely conditions:
- Critical information accumulation (takes 10⁶-10⁹ years)
- Presence at geometric weak point (localized, sparse)

Combined probability is extremely low. Expected rate: << 1 per galaxy per million years.

**Property 2: Instability**

Expelled material is boundary substrate—exotic matter foreign to both universes. Cannot achieve equilibrium with either universe's matter. Energetically unstable. Rapidly decays.

Additionally, rupture is self-limiting: expulsion relieves pressure, allowing boundary tension to reseal.

**Property 3: Brief Duration**

Rupture proceeds on **dynamical timescale** (seconds), not cosmological time. Expulsion continues until pressure drops below critical threshold (seconds to minutes). Exotic matter decay adds minutes to hours.

Total observable duration: seconds to hours, typically brief end of range.

**Property 4: Non-Observation**

Combining extreme rarity, brief duration, and unpredictable timing:
- Formation rate: < 10^-6 per galaxy per year
- Duration: seconds to minutes
- Observable fraction: depends on distance, orientation, background

Probability of catching one: vanishingly small without dedicated surveys.

### 2.4 Key Distinctions from Standard White Holes

**Standard interpretation:**
- White holes = time-reversed black holes
- Should be as common as black holes
- No formation mechanism
- Permanent structures

**Boundary rupture mechanism:**
- White holes = transient rupture events
- Extremely rare (formation timescale ~10⁶-10⁹ yr)
- Clear formation mechanism (accumulation → rupture)
- Transient phenomena (seconds to minutes)

This reinterpretation transforms white holes from "impossible eternal objects" to "rare brief events"—reconciling theory with non-observation.

## 4. Mathematical Formulation

### 4.1 Notation and Definitions

**Spatial Variables:**
- **x:** Position in 3D space (our universe/brane)
- **θ(x):** Boundary separation at position x (distance in extra dimension, brane gap, etc.)
- **∇²θ(x):** Laplacian (measures whether x is local minimum: ∇²θ > 0)

**Temporal Variables:**
- **t:** Time
- **t':** Integration variable (historical time)
- **τ_boundary:** Characteristic dissipation timescale for trapped information

**Physical Quantities:**
- **θ_residue(x,t):** Accumulated trapped information density at x, time t
- **f_trap:** Fraction of information trapped at black holes (~10^-5 to 10^-3)
- **Ṁ_BH:** Information flow through nearby black holes (mass accretion rate)
- **ρ_boundary:** Energy/mass density of boundary material
- **V_rupture:** Volume of rupture zone

**Dimensionless Parameters:**
- **θ_critical:** Critical information density for rupture
- **α_convergence:** Coupling constant governing runaway instability
- **α_eject:** Ejection amplification factor

### 4.2 Equation 1: Information Residue Accumulation

θ_residue(x,t) = ∫₀ᵗ [f_trap · Ṁ_BH(x,t') · e^(-(t-t')/τ_boundary) · Σᵢ δ_source_i(x,t')] dt'

**Physical interpretation:**

This integral tracks trapped information accumulation over cosmic time. Let's parse each term:

**f_trap · Ṁ_BH(x,t'):**  
At time t' and position x, black holes process information at rate Ṁ_BH. A fraction f_trap gets stuck in boundary. This is the **source term**.

**e^(-(t-t')/τ_boundary):**  
Information trapped at historical time t' decays exponentially. Some slowly dissipates back into universes with characteristic time τ_boundary ~ 10⁹-10¹² years. This is the **decay term**.

**Σᵢ δ_source_i(x,t'):**  
Sum over all wave sources i at each moment t'. Each δ_source_i represents a stellar event (supernova, jet activation, pulsar burst, merger, etc.) creating boundary waves that **transport** trapped information.

**Integration ∫₀ᵗ ... dt':**  
Sum contributions from all historical times t' = 0 (early universe) to t' = t (present).

**Physical picture:**  
Imagine a bathtub where:
- Faucet = black holes constantly adding droplets (trapped information)
- Drain (slow) = exponential decay (e^(-t/τ) term)
- Hand swirling water = wave sources moving droplets around (Σ δ_source terms)

After billions of years, certain regions accumulate high droplet density (θ_residue large).

**Dimensional analysis:**
[θ_residue] = [Information density] = [Mass] / [Length]³ (in units where ℏ=c=1)

### 4.3 Equation 2: White Hole Formation Condition

**Condition 1 (Pressure):** θ_residue(x) > θ_critical  
**Condition 2 (Geometry):** ∇²θ(x) > 0

White hole forms only when **both** conditions simultaneously satisfied.

**Condition 1 interpretation:**  
Sufficient accumulated "pressure" (information density) to potentially cause rupture. Like water pressure behind dam reaching structural limit.

**Condition 2 interpretation:**  
Position x is a **local minimum** in boundary thickness. Mathematically: ∇²θ = ∂²θ/∂x² + ∂²θ/∂y² + ∂²θ/∂z² > 0 means x is a basin (weak point).

**Why both required?**

Consider four scenarios:

| Scenario | θ_residue | ∇²θ | Result |
|----------|-----------|-----|--------|
| A | High | Negative | No rupture (thick boundary, no weak point) |
| B | High | Positive | **RUPTURE** (high pressure + weak point) |
| C | Low | Positive | No rupture (weak point but insufficient pressure) |
| D | Low | Negative | No rupture (neither condition) |

Only scenario B produces white hole.

**Probability estimate:**

Fraction of space satisfying Condition 2: ε_geom ~ 10^-3 (weak points are rare)  
Fraction of time satisfying Condition 1: ε_time ~ 10^-6 (accumulation takes ~10⁹ yr)  
Combined probability: ε_total ~ ε_geom · ε_time ~ 10^-9

This explains extreme rarity: only 1 in billion (space-time) points meets conditions.

### 4.4 Equation 3: Rupture Dynamics

θ_rupture(x) = θ_residue(x) / (1 - α_convergence/θ(x))

**This equation describes the runaway instability.**

**Physical interpretation:**

As rupture begins, θ(x) decreases (boundary thins). Let's trace the feedback:

1. **Initial state:** θ = θ₀ (initial thickness)
2. **Small perturbation:** θ → θ₀ - δθ (slight thinning)
3. **Increased stress:** Stress ∝ θ_residue/θ increases
4. **Further thinning:** Stress causes more thinning: θ → θ₀ - 2δθ
5. **Accelerating:** Each step makes next step worse

**Critical point:** When θ → α_convergence, denominator → 0, rupture rate → ∞

**Mathematical behavior:**

For θ >> α_convergence:
θ_rupture ≈ θ_residue / (1 - 0) = θ_residue  
(Stable, no runaway)

For θ ≈ α_convergence + ε (small ε):
θ_rupture ≈ θ_residue / (ε/α_convergence) → ∞  
(Explosive instability)

**Timescale analysis:**

Rupture timescale: t_rupture ~ (θ - α_convergence) / (dθ/dt)

As θ → α_convergence: t_rupture → 0

Typical values: t_rupture ~ 10^-3 to 10^2 seconds (dynamical, not cosmological)

**Analogy:**  
Like avalanche on steep slope. Small rockfall (δθ) destabilizes more rocks. Accelerating cascade. Unstoppable once critical angle exceeded.

### 4.5 Equation 4: Mass Expulsion

M_WH = ρ_boundary · V_rupture · (1 + α_eject/θ_rupture)

**Physical interpretation:**

The mass ejected during white hole event has three contributions:

**Base mass: ρ_boundary · V_rupture**  
The boundary material itself in rupture volume. Like tearing fabric—fragments have fabric mass.

ρ_boundary depends on boundary's energy density (units: [Mass]/[Length]³)  
V_rupture depends on geometric extent of rupture (units: [Length]³)

**Amplification factor: (1 + α_eject/θ_rupture)**  
Thinner boundary at rupture → more violent expulsion → more mass accelerated.

As θ_rupture decreases:
- α_eject/θ_rupture increases
- More material gets expelled
- Higher ejection velocity

**Typical values:**

ρ_boundary ~ 10^-29 to 10^-26 g/cm³ (comparable to dark energy density?)  
V_rupture ~ (1-1000 km)³  
α_eject/θ_rupture ~ 0.1 to 10 (depends on rupture severity)

**Result:** M_WH ~ 10^-3 to 10^3 M☉ (solar masses)

**Energy release:**

E_WH = M_WH · c² ~ 10^50 to 10^54 erg

Compare to:
- Supernova: ~10^51 erg (comparable!)
- Short GRB: ~10^49-10^51 erg (consistent)
- Long GRB: ~10^51-10^54 erg (consistent)

### 5.4 Morphological Classification

**Prediction 4: Four Morphological Types**

Depending on dominant wave source, white holes exhibit different morphologies:

**Type A: Supernova-Dominated (Spherical)**
- Dominant source: Supernovae (spherical shock waves)
- Morphology: Spherical or near-spherical ejection
- Duration: Brief (~seconds)
- Energy: High (~10^51 erg)
- **Candidate:** Short gamma-ray bursts without merger signatures

**Type B: Pulsar-Dominated (Pulsed)**
- Dominant source: Pulsars (rhythmic beams)
- Morphology: Pulsed emission (on/off pattern)
- Duration: Longer (~minutes), potentially repeating
- Energy: Moderate (~10^49 erg per pulse)
- **Candidate:** Repeating fast radio bursts

**Type C: Jet-Dominated (Directional)**
- Dominant source: Black hole jets (sustained beams)
- Morphology: Highly directional, jet-like
- Duration: Variable (~seconds to hours)
- Energy: Very high (~10^52-10^54 erg)
- **Candidate:** Long GRBs without supernovae ("orphan" GRBs)

**Type D: Mixed/Chaotic (Irregular)**
- Multiple competing sources
- Morphology: Complex, irregular
- Duration: Unpredictable
- Energy: Variable
- **Candidate:** Anomalous transients (e.g., "Cow"-like events)

**Test:** Classify FRBs/GRBs/transients into these categories. If classification is clean and correlates with predicted environmental factors (pulsar density, jet sources, etc.), strong support for mechanism.

**Timeline:** 2025-2030 with large catalogs enabling statistical classification

### 5.5 Multi-Messenger Signatures

**Prediction 5A: Gravitational Waves + Electromagnetic**

White hole rupture creates boundary perturbations detectable as gravitational waves:

**Expected GW frequency:** f_GW ~ c/(2π·R_rupture) ~ 10^2 to 10^4 Hz

Where R_rupture ~ 1-1000 km (rupture zone size)

**GW strain amplitude:** h ~ (G/c⁴) · (M_WH/D) · (v_rupture/c)²

h ~ 10^-23 to 10^-22 (marginally detectable by LIGO at design sensitivity)

**Distinguishing feature:** GW waveform differs from known sources:
- Not inspiral (black hole mergers)
- Not ringdown (post-merger)
- Unique signature: sudden onset + rapid decay

**Test:** Search for GW transients coincident with FRBs/GRBs

**Timeline:** 2027-2035 with LIGO/Virgo O5+ runs + Einstein Telescope

**Prediction 5B: Neutrinos**

Exotic matter decay should produce neutrinos:

**Expected flux:** Φ_ν ~ (E_WH/E_ν) · (1/4πD²) · f_ν

Where:
- E_ν ~ 1-100 GeV (typical neutrino energy)
- f_ν ~ 0.01-0.1 (fraction of energy in neutrinos)

For E_WH ~ 10^51 erg, D ~ 100 Mpc:
Φ_ν ~ 10^-2 to 10^-1 neutrinos/m² (detectable!)

**Test:** Search for neutrino transients coincident with FRBs/GRBs

**Timeline:** 2025-2035 with IceCube, KM3NeT

**Prediction 5C: Ultra-High-Energy Cosmic Rays**

Exotic matter decay produces UHECRs with energies > 10^18 eV

**Expected correlation:** White hole locations should correlate with UHECR arrival directions (accounting for magnetic deflection)

**Test:** Cross-correlate Auger/Telescope Array UHECR catalog with FRB/GRB positions

**Timeline:** 2025-2030 with growing UHECR samples

### 5.6 Null Tests (What Would Falsify This Mechanism?)

**Falsification Criterion 1:** If FRBs/GRBs show **no correlation** with black hole density or stellar activity regions (r < 0.1), mechanism is likely wrong.

**Falsification Criterion 2:** If spectroscopy of transient candidates shows **purely stellar** element/isotope ratios with no exotic signatures, boundary material interpretation is falsified.

**Falsification Criterion 3:** If white hole formation rate is **too high** (multiple events per galaxy per year), conflicts with mechanism's predicted rarity.

**Falsification Criterion 4:** If event durations are consistently **very long** (hours to days), inconsistent with dynamical rupture timescales.

**Falsification Criterion 5:** If multi-messenger searches find **no GW or neutrino coincidences** after 1000+ FRB detections, boundary rupture interpretation is unlikely.

**Timeline for falsification:** 2030 with sufficient statistical samples

Several classes of unexplained transient phenomena are consistent with white hole predictions. We examine three:

**Observational properties:**

- No clear progenitor identified

**White hole interpretation:**

**One-off FRBs = Type A (Supernova-Dominated)**
- Single rupture event
- Brief duration
- Spherical emission (isotropic radio)
- Consistent with M_WH ~ 10^-3 M☉ ruptures

**Repeating FRBs = Type B (Pulsar-Dominated)**
- Rhythmic boundary waves cause periodic ruptures at same weak point

- Multiple pulses as waves re-converge
- Consistent with M_WH ~ 10^-4 M☉ per event

**Tests:**

*Test 1:* Spatial distribution  
Compare FRB host galaxies to:
- Stellar mass (proxy for black hole density)

- **Expected:** Correlation with r > 0.4

*Test 2:* Repeater periodicity  
Search for pulsar-period correlations in repeating FRBs
- **Expected:** Period matches or harmonics of nearby pulsars

*Test 3:* Spectroscopy

- **Expected:** Non-stellar element ratios

**Current status:**

**Verdict:** **Plausible candidates**, requires further testing

### 6.2 Orphan Gamma-Ray Bursts

**Observational properties:**
- Long GRBs (>2 seconds) without associated supernovae
- Short GRBs without neutron star merger signatures
- Some show unusual spectral features
- Energy: 10^49-10^54 erg (consistent with M_WH ~ 10^-1 to 10^3 M☉)

**White hole interpretation:**

**Long orphan GRBs = Type C (Jet-Dominated)**
- Sustained black hole jet waves create directional rupture

- Duration: seconds to minutes

**Tests:**

- **Expected:** Power-law deviations, unusual temporal evolution

*Test 3:* Host galaxy properties

**Current status:**

**Verdict:** **Strong candidates**, particularly for jet-dominated Type C

**Verdict:** **Possible but uncertain**, requires detailed characterization
