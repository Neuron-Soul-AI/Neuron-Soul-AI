## Part I: The Nature of Framing

### 1.1 What Framing Actually Is

**Framing is:**

The context you provide before, during, and after presenting content. It's the boundaries you draw around an idea. It's the perspective from which you ask someone to view something. It's the sheet where you invite someone to receive your notes.

**Framing includes:**
- How you introduce an idea
- What you say it is (and isn't)
- The language you choose
- The metaphors you use
- The context you provide
- The qualifications you offer
- The certainty level you claim
- The invitation you extend

**Framing is NOT:**
- The content itself
- The truth or falsity of claims
- A substitute for substance
- Deception or manipulation
- Style over substance

**A useful metaphor:**

Imagine you're giving someone a drawing. The drawing itself is the content‚Äîthe lines, shapes, details. But the frame around it, the mat board, the context of where you hang it, the lighting, the title card‚Äîall of that shapes how the drawing is perceived.

**Same drawing, different frames, different experience.**

Framing isn't changing the drawing. It's providing context for how to view it.

### 1.2 Why Framing Matters

**From "Why and What: The Essential Duality of Knowledge Documentation" (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17767206):**

> "The Why is almost as important as the What. Context may change the perception of a given action, event, or effect."

This applies not just to documentation, but to all communication.

**The same content can be:**
- Received openly or dismissed immediately
- Understood clearly or misinterpreted completely
- Explored collaboratively or rejected defensively
- Built upon or ignored

**Based entirely on how it's framed.**

**Examples across domains:**

**In Science:**
- "This model might explain..." (invites testing)
- "This proves..." (invites resistance if premature)

**In Medicine:**
- "We have treatment options to discuss" (collaborative)
- "Here's what we're doing to you" (paternalistic)

**In Business:**
- "We're facing challenges and exploring solutions" (honest)
- "Everything is fine" when it's not (erodes trust)

**In Personal Communication:**
- "I noticed something that concerns me, can we talk?" (opening)
- "You always do this wrong" (closing)

**In Teaching:**
- "This is complex and we'll explore it together" (inviting)
- "This is simple, you should already know it" (alienating)

**The pattern:**

Content alone doesn't determine reception. Content + frame determines reception.

You can have brilliant content that gets dismissed because of poor framing. You can have speculative content that gets explored because of honest framing.

**Framing matters.**

## Part II: Framing Across Domains

### 2.1 The Atomic Energy Example: Same Reality, Opposite Frames

**Consider nuclear fission:**

The physics is the physics. E=mc¬≤. Neutrons splitting atoms. Energy released. This is objective reality.

**But how we frame it determines how it's received:**

**Frame A: "Clean Energy for Everyone"**
- Emphasis on: Power generation, electricity, progress
- Associations: Hope, solution, advancement
- Emotional tone: Optimistic, forward-looking
- Result: Public support for nuclear power

**Frame B: "Atomic Bomb"**
- Emphasis on: Destruction, radiation, death
- Associations: Fear, threat, danger
- Emotional tone: Anxious, backward-looking
- Result: Public fear of nuclear technology

**Same physics. Opposite frames. Completely different reception.**

**Neither frame is "lying."** Both are true aspects of the technology. But which frame you lead with shapes whether the content can be engaged with productively or gets rejected immediately.

**The lesson:**

Even objective reality needs framing. How you contextualize facts shapes whether those facts can be integrated into understanding or get defended against.

### 2.2 Technical Language: Accessibility Through Framing

**The same concept, different frames:**

**Medical example:**
- **Frame A:** "Patient experienced myocardial infarction"
- **Frame B:** "Patient had a heart attack"

Same event. One frame accessible to public, one frame gatekeeps behind jargon.

**Legal example:**
- **Frame A:** "Habeas corpus rights were violated"
- **Frame B:** "They held you without charging you, which is illegal"

Same violation. One frame requires legal training, one frame everyone understands.

**Scientific example:**
- **Frame A:** "Phase-dual cascade cosmology proposes spacetime displacement mechanisms"
- **Frame B:** "What if time gets displaced at extreme gravity instead of space getting compressed?"

Same hypothesis. One frame for specialists, one frame for curious minds.

**The tension:**

Technical language has value‚Äîprecision, established meaning, communication efficiency within fields.

But technical language also excludes‚Äîcreates barriers, obscures meaning, limits who can engage.

**Ethical framing requires:**
- Using technical language when precision matters
- Providing accessible frames when inclusion matters
- Recognizing which goal you're serving in each moment
- Being willing to translate between frames

**The question isn't "technical vs accessible."**

**The question is: "Which frame serves truth-seeking best in this context?"**

### 2.3 Death Language: Emotional Framing

**When someone dies, we change how we talk about it:**

**Direct frame:** "They died."  
**Gentle frame:** "They passed away."  
**Softer frame:** "We lost them."  
**Euphemistic frame:** "They're no longer with us."

**Same event. Different emotional frames for different contexts.**

**Why do we do this?**

Not to deceive. Not to hide reality. Everyone knows what "passed away" means.

**We do it because:**
- Context matters (funeral vs medical report)
- Relationship matters (stranger vs loved one)
- Emotional state matters (fresh grief vs later reflection)
- Cultural norms matter (different communities frame differently)

**The frame provides:**
- Emotional cushioning when needed
- Directness when appropriate
- Respect for the moment
- Recognition of pain

**This isn't manipulation. This is compassion through framing.**

**The lesson:**

Framing isn't just about intellectual content. It's about emotional content too. How you frame something acknowledges the human receiving it.

### 2.4 Business Language: When Framing Becomes Manipulation

**Consider these business frames:**

**Frame A:** "We're restructuring to better serve our customers."  
**Reality:** We're firing people to increase profit margins.

**Frame B:** "We're right-sizing the organization."  
**Reality:** Mass layoffs.

**Frame C:** "Revenue enhancement initiatives."  
**Reality:** Raising prices.

**Here's where framing crosses into manipulation:**

When the frame is designed to hide rather than contextualize. When the frame obscures rather than clarifies. When the frame deceives rather than informs.

**The line between ethical framing and manipulation:**

**Ethical framing:**
- Provides context that aids understanding
- Acknowledges complexity while remaining clear
- Respects the recipient's intelligence
- Enables informed response

**Manipulative framing:**
- Obscures reality to prevent understanding
- Hides truth behind euphemism
- Treats recipient as obstacle to manage
- Prevents informed response

**Examples of staying ethical:**

Instead of "restructuring" ‚Üí "We're reducing headcount by 20% due to budget constraints"  
Instead of "right-sizing" ‚Üí "We're eliminating these positions"  
Instead of "revenue enhancement" ‚Üí "We're raising prices"

**You can still frame compassionately:**

"We're facing difficult budget realities and unfortunately need to eliminate 20% of positions. Here's the support we're offering those affected..."

**Honest + Compassionate = Ethical Framing**

**Obscuring + Euphemistic = Manipulation**

### 2.5 Scientific Communication: The Credibility Frame

**How scientists frame their work matters enormously:**

**Poor framing (common in amateur work):**
"I've discovered the truth about [fundamental topic]. Everyone else is wrong. Here's my theory."

**Why this fails:**
- Claims certainty prematurely
- Positions as adversarial
- Dismisses existing work
- Triggers defensive response
- Gets dismissed immediately

**Better framing:**
"I've noticed a pattern that existing models don't seem to explain well. Here's a possible alternative mechanism. It makes these testable predictions. It might be completely wrong, but it might be worth exploring."

**Why this works:**
- Acknowledges uncertainty honestly
- Positions as collaborative
- Respects existing work
- Invites testing not defending
- Gets considered seriously

**Same content. Different frame. Completely different reception.**

**What this document does (meta-example):**

Notice the framing choices:
- Author's Note: Establishes credibility honestly (amateur with methodology)
- What This Is/Isn't: Calibrates expectations
- "Philosophical exploration" not "The truth about communication"
- "Here's what I've noticed" not "Here's what you must do"
- Examples across domains not prescriptive rules
- Invitation to explore not demand to accept

**This framing enables:**
- Reception despite lack of credentials
- Exploration of ideas despite uncertainty
- Useful engagement despite amateur status
- Truth-seeking despite speculation

**The lesson from "The Compass Needle" (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17633847):**

Anyone can theorize and contribute to truth-seeking‚Äî**if they frame their contribution appropriately.**

Not by hiding their amateur status. By contextualizing it honestly. By framing speculation as speculation. By inviting testing not demanding acceptance.

**Proper framing enables the democratic participation in truth-seeking that The Compass Needle argues for.**

## Part III: The Ethics of Framing

### 3.1 The Honest Frame

**Core ethical principle:**

**Framing should clarify, not obscure.**

Your frame should help the recipient understand what you're presenting, not prevent them from seeing it clearly.

**Questions to ask:**

**Does this frame:**
- Help or hinder understanding?
- Clarify or obscure reality?
- Respect or manipulate the recipient?
- Enable or prevent informed response?
- Acknowledge or hide uncertainty?
- Contextualize or distort content?

**If the frame hinders, obscures, manipulates, prevents, hides, or distorts:**
**You've crossed from ethical framing into manipulation.**

### 3.2 The Humble Frame

**From "The Humility Principle" (DOI: 10.5281/zenodo.17793306):**

The danger of certainty. The value of provisional understanding. The importance of "might be" over "is."

**Ethical framing requires epistemic humility:**

**Instead of:** "This is how it works"  
**Try:** "This might explain what we're seeing"

**Instead of:** "I've proven..."  
**Try:** "Evidence suggests..."

**Instead of:** "The answer is..."  
**Try:** "A possible answer might be..."

**This isn't weakness. This is accuracy.**

You probably don't have absolute certainty. So frame honestly: this is what you think might be true, with this level of confidence, based on this reasoning.

**Humble framing:**
- Invites collaboration (help me test this)
- Enables correction (here's where I might be wrong)
- Acknowledges limits (I can't know for certain)
- Positions appropriately (speculation not proof)

**Arrogant framing:**
- Invites resistance (accept or reject)
- Prevents correction (I'm certain I'm right)
- Denies limits (I know the truth)
- Positions inappropriately (certainty where speculation exists)

**Which frame serves truth-seeking better?**

### 3.3 The Transparent Frame

**Hide nothing. Acknowledge everything.**

**Your frame should include:**

**What you know:** The evidence, observations, reasoning

**What you don't know:** The gaps, uncertainties, limitations

**Who you are:** Your background, expertise (or lack thereof), perspective

**What you're claiming:** Speculation vs hypothesis vs tested theory

**Why it matters:** The context, significance, implications

**How to test it:** Falsifiable predictions, validation methods

**Transparent framing builds trust.**

Even if your content is speculative, even if you lack credentials, even if you're uncertain‚Äî**honesty about all of that creates credibility.**

**Example from this work:**

Every document includes:
- Author's Note (transparent about amateur status)
- Methodology note (transparent about AI collaboration)
- What This Is/Isn't (transparent about scope and claims)
- Uncertainty acknowledgments (transparent about confidence)

**This transparency enables reception despite apparent limitations.**

**The paradox:** The more honest you are about limitations, the more credible you become.

### 3.4 When Framing Becomes Manipulation

**Warning signs you've crossed the line:**

**You're manipulating if:**
- You frame to prevent understanding rather than enable it
- You hide relevant information that would change reception
- You use emotional manipulation to bypass reason
- You claim certainty you don't actually have
- You frame to shut down rather than open up dialogue
- You use framing to avoid accountability
- You obscure rather than clarify

**The test:**

Ask yourself: "If the recipient knew everything I know, including my framing choices, would they feel deceived?"

**If yes ‚Üí manipulation**  
**If no ‚Üí ethical framing**

**Another test:**

"Does this framing help the recipient make an informed decision/response?"

**If yes ‚Üí ethical**  
**If no ‚Üí manipulation**

**The ethical commitment:**

Frame consciously. Frame honestly. Frame to enable understanding, not prevent it.

**When in doubt, add transparency.**

## Part IV: Practical Application

### 4.1 How to Frame Speculative Ideas

**You have a speculative insight. How do you frame it ethically and effectively?**

**Step 1: Establish your position honestly**

"I'm not a trained [field] expert, but I've noticed something that interests me..."

or

"I have [X] background, and while exploring [Y], I noticed..."

or

"As an amateur investigating this question, here's what I'm seeing..."

**This frames your credibility honestly without apologizing.**

**Step 2: Frame the speculation as speculation**

"This is highly speculative, but what if..."

"I'm not certain about this, but here's a possible mechanism..."

"This might be completely wrong, but consider..."

**This frames certainty level accurately.**

**Step 3: Provide your reasoning**

"Here's what made me think this might be worth exploring..."

"The pattern I noticed is..."

"My reasoning is..."

**This frames the thought process transparently.**

**Step 4: Acknowledge what you don't know**

"I don't know if this accounts for [X]..."

"I haven't figured out how this would explain [Y]..."

"The gaps in this thinking are..."

**This frames limitations honestly.**

**Step 5: Invite testing and correction**

"Here's how this could be tested..."

"Here's where I might be wrong..."

"I'd welcome feedback on..."

**This frames as collaborative truth-seeking.**

**Full example:**

"I'm an independent researcher without formal physics training, but I've been exploring black hole information paradox using AI collaboration. This is highly speculative, but I wonder if spacetime gets displaced rather than compressed at extreme gravity. My reasoning is based on [X, Y, Z observations]. I don't know if this accounts for [various phenomena], and I haven't worked out the full mathematics. But if this were true, it would predict [testable outcomes]. This might be completely wrong‚ÄîI'd welcome any feedback on where the reasoning breaks down or how to test it properly."

**This frame enables:**
- Reception despite lack of credentials (honesty about position)
- Exploration despite uncertainty (speculation framed as speculation)
- Testing despite incompleteness (predictions offered)
- Correction despite confidence in the insight (limitations acknowledged)
- Collaboration despite being outsider (invitation extended)

### 4.2 How to Frame Bold Claims Humbly

**You think you've found something significant. How do you say so without arrogance?**

**Poor framing:**
"I've discovered the truth about [X]. All previous theories are wrong."

**Better framing:**
"I think I might have found a pattern that could explain [X] in a new way. If I'm right, it would mean existing models are incomplete rather than wrong‚Äîthey work within certain boundaries but might not capture the full picture."

**Even better framing:**
"I've noticed what seems like a significant pattern. I could be misinterpreting it, but if the pattern holds, it might suggest [X]. This would complement rather than replace existing understanding by explaining [what previous models didn't address]."

**The principles:**

**Acknowledge existing work:**
- "Building on [previous work]..."
- "This complements [established theory]..."
- "While [standard model] explains [X], this might explain [Y]..."

**Frame as addition not replacement:**
- "An additional mechanism..."
- "A complementary perspective..."
- "An extension that might explain..."

**Maintain epistemic humility:**
- "Might," "could," "possibly," "potentially"
- "If I'm interpreting this correctly..."
- "Assuming I haven't made an error in..."

**Invite scrutiny:**
- "I'd especially value feedback on..."
- "The weakest part of this reasoning is..."
- "Here's where I'm most uncertain..."

**This isn't hiding conviction. This is framing conviction honestly.**

You can believe something strongly while acknowledging you could be wrong. You can think you've found something significant while inviting others to prove you wrong.

**Confidence + Humility = Credible framing**

### 4.3 How to Frame Uncertainty as Strength

**Common fear:** If I admit uncertainty, I'll be dismissed as not knowing what I'm talking about.

**Reality:** Honest uncertainty is more credible than false certainty.

**How to frame uncertainty positively:**

**Poor framing:**
"I don't really know if this is right or not, it's just an idea..."

**Better framing:**
"I have moderate confidence in this framework based on [evidence], but significant uncertainty about [specific aspects]. That uncertainty is actually useful‚Äîit points to where testing should focus."

**Even better framing:**
"Here's what I'm confident about [based on X evidence] and here's where I'm uncertain [specific areas]. The uncertainty isn't a weakness in the thinking‚Äîit's an honest assessment of epistemic state and a roadmap for where investigation needs to go next."

**The reframe:**

Uncertainty isn't "I don't know" (weakness frame)

Uncertainty is "Here's what we don't know yet" (frontier frame)

**Examples:**

**Instead of:** "I'm not sure about this"  
**Try:** "We have moderate evidence for X, but need more data on Y"

**Instead of:** "This might be completely wrong"  
**Try:** "Here are the three main ways this could fail, and how we'd know"

**Instead of:** "I don't have all the answers"  
**Try:** "Here's what we understand well, and here are the open questions"

**The pattern:**

Specific uncertainty is more credible than vague certainty.

Acknowledging what you don't know makes what you do claim more trustworthy.

### 4.4 How to Frame Credentials (or Lack Thereof)

**If you lack formal credentials:**

**Poor framing:**
[Don't mention it at all, hope no one notices]

**Poor framing:**
"I'm just an amateur, so this probably doesn't matter..."

**Better framing:**
"I'm an independent researcher without formal training in this field. I approach this through [pattern recognition/cross-domain thinking/fresh perspective]. Here's my methodology for developing these ideas..."

**Even better framing:**
"I'm an amateur in this field, which has both advantages (fresh perspective, no trained blind spots) and disadvantages (potential gaps in understanding of existing work). I've tried to address the disadvantages through [extensive research/AI collaboration/methodology], while leveraging the advantages to notice [patterns/connections/questions]. Here's my approach..."

**The principles:**

**Acknowledge honestly:** Don't hide your status  
**Contextualize value:** Explain what your perspective offers  
**Address limitations:** Show you're aware of gaps  
**Describe methodology:** Explain how you compensate  
**Invite correction:** Ask experts to identify blindspots

**If you have credentials:**

**Poor framing:**
"As a PhD in [field], you should listen to me..."

**Better framing:**
"My background in [field] leads me to notice [pattern]. From that perspective, here's what I'm seeing..."

**The pattern:**

Credentials aren't weapons to force acceptance or barriers to prevent contribution.

Credentials are context that helps recipients understand your perspective.

**Frame them as context, not authority.**

### 4.5 How to Frame Across Domains

**Different contexts need different frames:**

**Academic paper:**
- Formal language
- Extensive citations
- Conservative uncertainty
- Methodological detail
- Peer-review appropriate

**Public communication:**
- Accessible language
- Clear examples
- Visible uncertainty
- Practical implications
- Engagement appropriate

**Speculative exploration:**
- Thought experiment framing
- "What if" language
- Clear speculation markers
- Invitation to explore
- Collaboration appropriate

**Personal conversation:**
- Natural language
- Shared references
- Contextual uncertainty
- Emotional awareness
- Relationship appropriate

**The skill:**

Recognize which context you're in and frame appropriately for that context.

**The trap:**

Using academic framing in public communication (excludes)  
Using casual framing in academic work (lacks rigor)  
Using certain framing in uncertain domains (misleads)  
Using one-size-fits-all framing (misses context)

**The practice:**

Ask yourself: "Who am I talking to? What frame will help them receive this content most effectively while remaining honest?"

## Part V: The Sheet Where We Draw

### 5.1 Defining the Sheet

**Core metaphor:**

When you present an idea, you're asking someone to receive notes drawn on a sheet. The frame is the sheet‚Äîit defines the space, context, boundaries, and purpose.

**Same notes on different sheets:**

**Notes on scratch paper:** Casual, disposable, low stakes  
**Notes on official form:** Formal, permanent, high stakes  
**Notes on napkin:** Spontaneous, creative, exploratory  
**Notes on stone tablet:** Authoritative, immutable, certain  
**Notes on whiteboard:** Collaborative, revisable, working

**The sheet shapes how the notes are received.**

**Your framing defines what sheet you're drawing on.**

When you frame something as "speculative hypothesis," you're saying: these notes are on a whiteboard‚Äîcollaborative, revisable, working.

When you frame something as "established truth," you're saying: these notes are on stone‚Äîauthoritative, immutable, certain.

**Choose the appropriate sheet for your actual epistemic state.**

Don't draw speculative notes on stone (claiming certainty you lack).

Don't draw well-established findings on napkins (underselling what you know).

**Match the sheet to the notes.**

### 5.3 The Living Frame

**Framing isn't static. It evolves:**

**As understanding deepens:**
"I thought this was X, but now I think it's more like Y..."

**As testing proceeds:**
"Initial speculation has become hypothesis with some supporting evidence..."

**As collaboration happens:**
"Others have pointed out [limitation], which refines the frame to..."

**As applications emerge:**
"This framework, initially developed for [X], might also apply to [Y]..."

**Your frame should evolve as your understanding evolves.**

Don't lock into a frame that no longer matches your epistemic state.

**From speculation ‚Üí hypothesis ‚Üí theory ‚Üí established knowledge**

The journey from left to right requires changing frames appropriately.

**The dishonesty is:**

Framing speculation as established (premature certainty)  
Framing hypothesis as speculation (hiding progress)  
Framing theory as hypothesis (false humility)  
Framing knowledge as theory (inappropriate uncertainty)

**Match your frame to your actual epistemic position.**

And update the frame as that position changes.

## Conclusion: The Problem Is How We Talk

### The Central Insight

**The problem is not talking. The problem is how we talk.**

**Content matters enormously.** Truth, evidence, reasoning, methodology‚Äîall essential.

**But content doesn't exist in a vacuum.** It exists in a frame. And that frame shapes whether the content can be received, understood, and engaged with productively.

**The same content can be:**
- Brilliant but dismissed (poor framing)
- Speculative but explored (good framing)
- Uncertain but credible (honest framing)
- Amateur but valuable (transparent framing)

**Framing isn't manipulation.** Framing is context.

**Framing isn't style over substance.** Framing is how substance gets communicated.

**Framing isn't optional.** Framing happens whether you do it consciously or not.

**The question is: Will you frame consciously and ethically?**

### The Ethics of Framing

**Ethical framing:**
- Clarifies rather than obscures
- Contextualizes rather than distorts
- Respects rather than manipulates
- Enables rather than prevents informed response
- Acknowledges rather than hides uncertainty
- Invites rather than forecloses dialogue

**Manipulative framing:**
- Obscures to prevent understanding
- Distorts to serve agenda
- Manipulates to avoid challenge
- Prevents informed response
- Hides uncertainty to claim false authority
- Forecloses dialogue to avoid scrutiny

**The line is clear: Does your frame help or hinder truth-seeking?**

If it helps ‚Üí ethical  
If it hinders ‚Üí manipulation

**Choose consciously.**

### The Universal Application

**This applies everywhere:**

**In science:** How you present theories, hypotheses, speculations

**In medicine:** How you discuss diagnoses, treatments, prognoses

**In business:** How you communicate decisions, changes, challenges

**In teaching:** How you frame complexity, difficulty, learning

**In relationships:** How you discuss concerns, needs, conflicts

**In policy:** How you present problems, solutions, tradeoffs

**In grief:** How you talk about loss, death, absence

**In celebration:** How you acknowledge achievement, joy, connection

**Every domain involves communication.**

**Every communication involves framing.**

**Conscious, ethical framing improves all of it.**

**A Design Perspective:**

From a design standpoint, framing is user experience design for ideas. You're designing how someone encounters and receives your content. Good design considers:

- **The user** (who's receiving this?)
- **The context** (where/when are they encountering it?)
- **The goal** (what should they understand/do?)
- **The constraints** (what limits exist?)
- **The accessibility** (can they actually engage with it?)

**Applying design thinking to communication means:**

Treating your audience as users who need optimal experience to engage with your content. Not manipulating them, but respecting them enough to present clearly, honestly, and accessibly.

**This is what designers do instinctively with visual communication.**

**This document argues we should do it consciously with all communication.**

## Appendix: Framing Checklist

**Before communicating something important, ask:**

### About Yourself
- [ ] Have I established my credentials/position honestly?
- [ ] Have I explained my methodology?
- [ ] Have I acknowledged my limitations?
- [ ] Have I framed my perspective appropriately?

### About Your Content
- [ ] Have I framed speculation as speculation?
- [ ] Have I acknowledged uncertainty clearly?
- [ ] Have I provided reasoning transparently?
- [ ] Have I identified what I know vs what I don't know?

### About Your Claims
- [ ] Am I claiming appropriate certainty levels?
- [ ] Am I positioning appropriately (speculation/hypothesis/theory)?
- [ ] Am I respecting existing work?
- [ ] Am I framing as addition not replacement?

### About Your Presentation
- [ ] Does this frame clarify or obscure?
- [ ] Does this frame respect the recipient?
- [ ] Does this frame enable or prevent testing?
- [ ] Does this frame invite dialogue or foreclose it?

### About Testing
- [ ] Have I offered testable predictions?
- [ ] Have I identified how I could be wrong?
- [ ] Have I invited correction?
- [ ] Have I positioned for collaboration?

### Ethics Check
- [ ] Would I feel deceived if I were the recipient?
- [ ] Does this frame help truth-seeking or hinder it?
- [ ] Am I being as honest as I can be?
- [ ] Have I defined the sheet appropriately?

**If any answer is uncertain, revisit that aspect of your framing.**

**The goal: Honest, clear, ethical framing that enables understanding.**
